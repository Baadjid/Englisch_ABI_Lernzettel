1. Introducing Opinions and Views

Expressing Your Own Opinion

‚ñº
In my opinion, ... / In my view, ...
In my opinion, social media has both positive and negative effects on society.
From my perspective, ... / From my point of view, ...
From my perspective, education is the key to solving many social problems.
I believe / think / feel / consider that ...
I believe that climate change is the most pressing issue of our time.
It seems to me that ... / It appears that ...
It seems to me that young people are more politically engaged than previous generations.
I am convinced that ... / I am of the opinion that ...
I am convinced that renewable energy will eventually replace fossil fuels.
To my mind, ... / As I see it, ...
To my mind, cultural diversity enriches society rather than dividing it.

Introducing Others' Opinions

‚ñº
According to (the author/the article/...), ...
According to the author, immigration has significantly shaped American culture.
The author argues / claims / suggests / maintains that ...
The author argues that technology has fundamentally changed how we communicate.
The writer points out / emphasizes / highlights that ...
The writer emphasizes that economic inequality remains a major challenge.
It is stated / mentioned / noted that ...
It is stated that over 40 million people live in poverty in the United States.
Some people believe / argue / claim that ...
Some people believe that globalization benefits everyone equally.

Expressing Certainty and Doubt

‚ñº

Expressing Certainty:

Undoubtedly, ... / Without doubt, ... / Certainly, ...
Undoubtedly, artificial intelligence will transform many industries.
It is clear / obvious / evident that ...
It is clear that climate change is already affecting global weather patterns.
There is no doubt that ... / Unquestionably, ...
There is no doubt that education plays a crucial role in social mobility.

Expressing Doubt or Reservation:

It is doubtful / questionable / uncertain whether ...
It is doubtful whether current policies will be sufficient to address the crisis.
One might question whether ... / It remains to be seen whether ...
It remains to be seen whether these reforms will have the desired effect.

2. Agreeing and Disagreeing

Agreeing

‚ñº
I (completely/entirely) agree with ... / I share this view.
I completely agree with the author's assessment of the situation.
This argument is convincing / persuasive / valid.
This argument is persuasive because it is supported by statistical evidence.
The author makes a good/valid point when he/she says that ...
The author makes a valid point when she says that social media can spread misinformation.
I support / endorse this viewpoint.
I support this viewpoint as it reflects current scientific consensus.

Disagreeing

‚ñº
I (completely) disagree with ... / I cannot agree with ...
I completely disagree with the notion that technology is inherently harmful.
I find this argument unconvincing / problematic / flawed.
I find this argument unconvincing because it ignores important counterevidence.
This view is questionable / debatable / contested.
This view is questionable given recent research findings.
I take issue with / I object to / I challenge ...
I take issue with the assumption that all immigrants face the same challenges.
Contrary to this claim, ... / On the contrary, ...
Contrary to this claim, evidence suggests that diversity strengthens communities.

Partial Agreement

‚ñº
While I agree that ..., I believe that ...
While I agree that technology has benefits, I believe we must also address its negative impacts.
To some extent / To a certain degree, ...
To some extent, the author is correct about the economic benefits of globalization.
I agree with X, but / however ...
I agree with the first point, but I have reservations about the proposed solution.
This is true / valid, but it should be noted that ...
This is valid, but it should be noted that the situation varies significantly by region.

3. Presenting Arguments and Evidence

Making Points

‚ñº
First of all, / Firstly, / In the first place, ...
First of all, we must consider the economic implications of this policy.
Secondly, / Furthermore, / Moreover, / In addition, ...
Furthermore, environmental concerns must be taken into account.
Another important point is that ... / It should also be mentioned that ...
Another important point is that education levels correlate with economic prosperity.
Finally, / Lastly, / Last but not least, ...
Last but not least, we must consider the human cost of these decisions.

Providing Evidence and Examples

‚ñº
For example, / For instance, / To illustrate, ...
For example, countries with strong social safety nets tend to have lower poverty rates.
This can be seen in / This is evident in / This is illustrated by ...
This is evident in the rising number of climate-related disasters.
Evidence suggests that / Research shows that / Studies indicate that ...
Research shows that bilingual education benefits cognitive development.
A good example of this is ... / A case in point is ...
A case in point is the success of renewable energy programs in Scandinavia.
Statistics reveal that / Data shows that / Figures indicate that ...
Statistics reveal that income inequality has increased significantly over the past decades.

Emphasizing and Highlighting

‚ñº
It is important / essential / crucial to note that ...
It is crucial to note that correlation does not imply causation.
Particularly / Especially / Most importantly, ...
Most importantly, these policies must be sustainable in the long term.
What is striking / remarkable / noteworthy is that ...
What is striking is that this problem affects both developed and developing nations.
It should be emphasized / stressed / highlighted that ...
It should be emphasized that education alone cannot solve all social problems.

4. Analyzing Texts

Introducing the Text

‚ñº
The text/article/extract titled "..." by ... deals with / focuses on / examines ...
The article titled "Climate Justice" by Sarah Green examines the disproportionate impact of climate change on developing nations.
The author discusses / explores / addresses the issue of ...
The author addresses the issue of racial inequality in the American criminal justice system.
The text was published in ... on ... and is aimed at ...
The text was published in The Guardian on March 15, 2024, and is aimed at a general readership.
The central theme / main topic of the text is ...
The central theme of the text is the tension between national security and personal freedom.

Analyzing Structure and Organization

‚ñº
The text is divided into / consists of / is structured in ...
The text is divided into three main sections, each addressing a different aspect of immigration policy.
In the opening paragraph, the author ...
In the opening paragraph, the author establishes the urgency of the climate crisis.
The author begins by / starts with / introduces the topic by ...
The author begins by presenting a personal anecdote that illustrates the broader issue.
The text moves on to / proceeds to / continues by ...
The text proceeds to examine the historical roots of the current situation.
In conclusion, / Finally, / To sum up, the author ...
In conclusion, the author calls for immediate action to address these challenges.

Analyzing Language and Style

‚ñº
The author uses / employs / makes use of ...
The author employs rhetorical questions to engage the reader and prompt reflection.
The tone of the text is (formal/informal/objective/subjective/critical/satirical/humorous/serious)
The tone of the text is critical yet balanced, presenting multiple perspectives.
The language is (descriptive/emotive/technical/accessible/complex/persuasive)
The language is accessible yet sophisticated, making complex ideas understandable.
The author appeals to / evokes / creates ...
The author appeals to the reader's sense of justice and moral responsibility.
Through the use of metaphors/imagery/statistics/anecdotes, the author ...
Through the use of vivid imagery, the author brings the human cost of the crisis to life.

Identifying Author's Purpose and Intention

‚ñº
The author's purpose is to inform / persuade / entertain / criticize / warn ...
The author's purpose is to persuade readers that urgent climate action is necessary.
The author intends to / aims to / seeks to ...
The author seeks to raise awareness about the plight of refugees.
By doing this, the author hopes to / wants to / attempts to ...
By presenting these statistics, the author wants to shock readers into action.

5. Describing Visuals (Graphs, Charts, Cartoons)

General Description

‚ñº
The graph/chart/diagram shows / illustrates / depicts / displays ...
The graph shows the steady increase in global temperatures over the past century.
The cartoon portrays / represents / satirizes ...
The cartoon satirizes politicians' failure to address climate change.
According to the chart/graph, / As can be seen from the diagram, ...
As can be seen from the diagram, unemployment rates vary significantly by education level.
The visual presents data on / provides information about / compares ...
The visual compares carbon emissions across different countries.

Describing Trends

‚ñº
There is a sharp/steady/gradual/dramatic increase/rise/growth in ...
There is a dramatic increase in the number of people using renewable energy.
There has been a decline/decrease/fall/drop in ...
There has been a steady decline in manufacturing jobs over the past decades.
The figure remains stable / stays constant / fluctuates / peaks at ...
The figure remains stable throughout the 1990s before increasing sharply.
X doubled/tripled / X rose/fell from ... to ... / X increased/decreased by ...
Immigration rates increased by 15% between 2010 and 2020.

Describing Cartoons

‚ñº
The cartoon consists of ... / In the foreground/background, we can see ...
In the foreground, we can see a politician with his head in the sand.
The figure/character represents / symbolizes / stands for ...
The character symbolizes the average citizen struggling with rising costs.
The cartoonist uses exaggeration/irony/satire to ...
The cartoonist uses exaggeration to highlight the absurdity of the situation.
The caption reads / says / states: "..."
The caption reads: "Meanwhile, in Washington..." adding another layer of irony.
The message/point of the cartoon is that ...
The message of the cartoon is that political leaders are ignoring obvious problems.

6. Discussing Issues and Problems

Identifying Problems

‚ñº
One of the main/major/key problems/issues/challenges is ...
One of the major challenges is ensuring equal access to quality education.
A significant concern is / A pressing issue is ...
A pressing issue is the growing wealth gap between rich and poor.
This raises the question of / This brings up the issue of ...
This raises the question of how to balance security with individual freedom.
There are concerns about / There is controversy surrounding ...
There is controversy surrounding the use of surveillance technology.

Discussing Advantages and Disadvantages

‚ñº
One advantage/benefit of ... is that ...
One advantage of globalization is increased economic interconnection.
A major/significant drawback/disadvantage is ...
A significant drawback is the loss of local jobs to overseas competition.
On the one hand, ... On the other hand, ...
On the one hand, technology connects people. On the other hand, it can create isolation.
While ... has its merits, it also has serious drawbacks
While social media has its merits in connecting people, it also has serious drawbacks in terms of privacy.

Proposing Solutions

‚ñº
A possible solution would be to ... / One way to address this is to ...
A possible solution would be to invest more heavily in renewable energy infrastructure.
This problem could be solved/tackled/addressed by ...
This problem could be addressed by implementing stricter environmental regulations.
It is necessary/essential/vital to ...
It is essential to ensure that all citizens have access to affordable healthcare.
Steps should be taken to / Measures must be implemented to ...
Steps should be taken to reduce carbon emissions significantly by 2030.

7. Writing Essays and Compositions

Introduction Phrases

‚ñº
In recent years, / Over the past decades, / Nowadays, ...
In recent years, climate change has become an increasingly urgent concern.
It is widely acknowledged/recognized that ...
It is widely acknowledged that education is crucial for economic development.
The question of whether ... has been a topic of much debate
The question of whether technology improves or harms society has been a topic of much debate.
This essay will discuss/examine/explore/analyze ...
This essay will examine the causes and consequences of income inequality.

Developing Paragraphs

‚ñº
To begin with, / To start with, / Initially, ...
To begin with, we must understand the historical context of the issue.
With regard to / Concerning / Regarding ...
Regarding environmental policy, there are several competing approaches.
Moving on to / Turning to / Let us now consider ...
Moving on to economic factors, we see that inequality has risen dramatically.

Concluding an Essay

‚ñº
To conclude, / In conclusion, / To sum up, ...
In conclusion, addressing climate change requires coordinated global action.
Taking everything into account, / All things considered, ...
All things considered, the benefits of immigration outweigh the challenges.
The evidence suggests that / It can be concluded that ...
The evidence suggests that early intervention programs significantly improve outcomes.
Looking to the future, / In the long term, ...
Looking to the future, sustainable policies will be essential for survival.

8. Comparing and Contrasting

Showing Similarities

‚ñº
Similarly, / Likewise, / In the same way, ...
Similarly, both countries face challenges integrating immigrant populations.
Both X and Y / X as well as Y ...
Both the United States and Great Britain have struggled with racial inequality.
X is similar to Y in that / X resembles Y in ...
The American Civil Rights Movement is similar to South African anti-apartheid struggle in its use of nonviolent resistance.
X and Y have ... in common / X shares ... with Y
Developed nations have rising inequality in common.

Showing Differences

‚ñº
However, / In contrast, / On the contrary, ...
However, the approaches to healthcare differ significantly between the two countries.
Whereas / While X ..., Y ...
Whereas Europe has universal healthcare, the United States relies on a private system.
Unlike X, Y ... / In contrast to X, Y ...
Unlike previous generations, today's young people are digital natives.
X differs from Y in that / The difference between X and Y is ...
The difference between the two approaches is that one prioritizes individual freedom while the other emphasizes collective welfare.
On the one hand ... on the other hand ...
On the one hand, globalization creates opportunities; on the other hand, it can increase inequality.

9. Expressing Cause and Effect

Indicating Causes

‚ñº
Because of / Due to / Owing to ...
Due to climate change, extreme weather events are becoming more frequent.
This is caused by / This results from / This stems from ...
This inequality stems from historical patterns of discrimination.
The reason for ... is that / ... can be attributed to ...
The reason for increased migration is that economic opportunities are unevenly distributed.
As a result of / As a consequence of ...
As a result of globalization, cultures are increasingly interconnected.

Indicating Effects

‚ñº
Therefore, / Thus, / Consequently, / As a result, ...
Therefore, immediate action is required to prevent further damage.
This leads to / This results in / This causes ...
This leads to increased social tensions and political instability.
The effect/consequence/result of ... is that ...
The consequence of ignoring these warnings is that problems will worsen.
This has led to / This has resulted in / This has given rise to ...
This has led to a fundamental transformation of the workplace.

10. Drawing Conclusions and Making Recommendations

Summarizing

‚ñº
In summary, / To summarize, / In brief, ...
In summary, the issue is complex and requires multifaceted solutions.
The main points are / The key findings are ...
The key findings are that education and economic opportunity are closely linked.
What emerges from this is / What becomes clear is ...
What becomes clear is that sustainable development requires balancing economic and environmental needs.

Making Recommendations

‚ñº
It is recommended that / It is advisable to / It would be beneficial to ...
It is recommended that governments invest in renewable energy infrastructure.
Action should be taken to / Efforts should be made to ...
Efforts should be made to ensure equal access to education for all children.
There is a need for / What is needed is ...
What is needed is a comprehensive approach addressing both causes and symptoms.

Looking to the Future

‚ñº
In the future, / In the long run, / In the coming years, ...
In the coming years, artificial intelligence will transform many industries.
If current trends continue, / Unless action is taken, ...
Unless action is taken, inequality will continue to widen.
It remains to be seen whether / Only time will tell if ...
It remains to be seen whether these policies will achieve their intended goals.

Letter Opening

Making Requests

‚ñº

Letter Closing

‚ñº
