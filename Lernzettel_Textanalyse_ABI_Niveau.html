üÜï 3. Analysing Argumentative Texts

‚ö†Ô∏è WICHTIG: Argumentative texts offer a lot of claims and theses, each of which is or should be supported by logical arguments as well as ethical and emotional appeals. Your task: Find out which types of arguments are used!

A) Types of Arguments

Type of Argument Description Example & Effect
Practical Argument Refers to widely known facts and common experience in order to appear logical "Humans need oxygen to survive and plants produce oxygen - that's why it is important to protect the environment."

Effect: Creates logical, undeniable foundation
Argument by Illustration Uses examples to support and illustrate a claim "The death penalty should be abolished because innocent people may be put to death. In 1990, for example, a man was executed in Florida for the murder of two police officers. Two years later he was found innocent."

Effect: Makes abstract claims concrete and relatable
Argument by Authority Uses the opinion of experts and the results of statistics to prove a point "Recent studies show that very frequent use of social media affects mental health."

Effect: Lends credibility through external validation
Normative Argument Refers to accepted norms and values. Often based on common sense. "To save our planet we will have to drastically reduce water and air pollution."

Effect: Appeals to shared values and ethics
Argument by Analogy The reader can easily draw parallels to other areas of life "In sports as well as in business, we can see globalisation, monopolies and illegal actions to 'win at all costs' increasing. Sport is more and more heavily regulated to ensure that competition is fair and equal, thus business should be too."

Effect: Makes complex issues understandable through familiar comparisons

B) Line of Argumentation

Analysing the line of argumentation: This includes describing and explaining how the argumentation is being developed throughout the text. How are the various parts of the text connected? What is the function of each individual paragraph?
Analysis Example:
"The author employs a deductive argumentation structure, beginning with the general thesis that climate change threatens humanity (paragraph 1), then narrowing to specific examples of environmental destruction (paragraphs 2-3), before concluding with concrete action steps (paragraph 4). This funnel-like structure guides readers from accepting the broad principle to committing to specific actions."

B) The Inverted Pyramid Structure

The Inverted Pyramid of Newspaper Articles

HEADLINE
Catches the reader's attention
LEAD PARAGRAPH
Introduces the news story by telling the most important facts (Who, What, When, Where, Why, How)
BODY - Important Details
More facts and additional details, quotes from witnesses/experts
TAIL - Background Info
Least important information, background context, related stories
Why the Inverted Pyramid? Readers can stop reading when they feel they have enough information. The most crucial information is at the top, so even if someone only reads the headline and lead, they understand the main story. Editors can also cut from the bottom if space is limited.
