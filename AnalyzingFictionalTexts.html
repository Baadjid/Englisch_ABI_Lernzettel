Setting

What is Setting?

‚ñº

The setting refers to the time and place where a story occurs. It creates the context and atmosphere for the narrative and can significantly influence characters, plot, and themes.

Components of Setting:

  • Time: Historical period, season, time of day
  • Place: Geographic location, specific locations (city, house, room)
  • Social Environment: Cultural, political, economic conditions
  • Atmosphere/Mood: The feeling created by the setting

Types of Setting

‚ñº

Temporal Setting (Time):

  • Historical Period: Victorian Era, WWII, future dystopia
  • Season: Winter's harshness, spring's renewal
  • Time of Day: Dawn (new beginnings), midnight (danger, secrets)
  • Duration: Single day, entire lifetime, generations

Spatial Setting (Place):

  • Geographic: Country, region, city vs. countryside
  • Specific Locations: House, school, battlefield
  • Real vs. Fictional: London vs. Middle-earth
  • Interior vs. Exterior: Confined spaces vs. open landscapes

Social Setting:

  • Class Structure: Aristocracy, working class, poverty
  • Cultural Norms: Traditions, values, expectations
  • Political Climate: Democracy, dictatorship, revolution
  • Economic Conditions: Prosperity, depression, inequality

Functions of Setting

‚ñº

1. Establishing Atmosphere/Mood:

  • Gothic: Gloomy castles, storms create suspense and fear
  • Romantic: Beautiful landscapes reflect emotions
  • Oppressive: Confined spaces increase tension
Example: In Wuthering Heights, the wild Yorkshire moors mirror the passionate, untamed nature of the characters.

2. Reflecting Character:

  • Settings can mirror characters' internal states
  • Characters' homes reveal personality traits
  • Relationship between character and environment shows values

3. Creating Conflict:

  • Man vs. Nature: Harsh environments challenge survival
  • Man vs. Society: Social settings create restrictions
  • Cultural Clash: Different settings represent competing values

4. Symbolism:

  • Settings can represent abstract concepts
  • Gardens: Paradise, innocence, cultivation
  • Wilderness: Freedom, danger, the unknown
  • Cities: Civilization, corruption, opportunity

5. Historical/Social Commentary:

  • Realistic settings critique society
  • Historical settings comment on past/present
  • Settings expose injustice, inequality

Analyzing Setting

‚ñº

Questions to Ask:

  • When and where does the story take place?
  • How detailed is the description of the setting?
  • What atmosphere/mood does the setting create?
  • How does the setting influence characters' behavior?
  • Does the setting change throughout the story? Why?
  • What symbolic meaning might the setting have?
  • How does the setting relate to the themes?
  • Is the setting realistic, fantastical, or symbolic?

Useful Vocabulary for Analysis:

Aspect Vocabulary
Atmosphere ominous, oppressive, serene, melancholic, vibrant, gloomy, idyllic, hostile
Time contemporary, historical, timeless, cyclical, linear progression
Place isolated, claustrophobic, expansive, exotic, familiar, urban, rural
Function symbolizes, reflects, foreshadows, contrasts with, reinforces, mirrors
Analysis Tip: Always connect setting analysis to other elements (character, plot, theme). Ask: "How does this setting contribute to the overall meaning of the work?"

Examples from Literature

‚ñº

Romeo and Juliet (Verona, Italy):

  • Public vs. Private: Violence in streets vs. intimacy in Juliet's balcony
  • Day vs. Night: Danger in daylight (feuding), safety in darkness (love)
  • Social Structure: Families' status creates conflict

Nineteen Eighty-Four (Oceania/London):

  • Totalitarian Setting: Constant surveillance, propaganda everywhere
  • Decaying London: Reflects deterioration of humanity and freedom
  • Ministry Buildings: Ironically named to show government control

The Great Gatsby (1920s New York):

  • East Egg vs. West Egg: Old money vs. new money
  • Valley of Ashes: Moral decay, forgotten working class
  • Gatsby's Mansion: Excessive wealth masking emptiness

Structure of a Classical Drama

Freytag's Pyramid

‚ñº

The classical dramatic structure, as analyzed by Gustav Freytag in the 19th century, describes the typical five-act structure of drama, particularly in tragedies.

The Five Acts:

1. Exposition (Introduction)

  • Introduces main characters, setting, and background information
  • Establishes the normal world before conflict begins
  • Provides necessary context for understanding the story
  • Inciting Incident: Event that disrupts equilibrium and starts the action
Example - Romeo and Juliet: The Montague-Capulet feud is introduced, Romeo is lovesick over Rosaline, Paris wants to marry Juliet. The inciting incident: Romeo crashes the Capulet party and meets Juliet.

2. Rising Action (Complication)

  • Conflict develops and intensifies
  • Complications arise, creating obstacles for protagonist
  • Tension and suspense build
  • Multiple plot developments occur
  • Character relationships evolve
Example - Romeo and Juliet: Secret marriage, Tybalt kills Mercutio, Romeo kills Tybalt and is banished, Juliet is ordered to marry Paris.

3. Climax (Turning Point)

  • The highest point of tension and drama
  • Point of No Return: Decisive moment determining the outcome
  • Protagonist faces their greatest challenge
  • In tragedy: Hero's downfall becomes inevitable
  • In comedy: Resolution becomes possible
Example - Romeo and Juliet: Juliet takes the sleeping potion. This decision sets in motion the tragic ending - there's no turning back.

4. Falling Action (Return/Reversal)

  • Events following the climax
  • Consequences of climactic decision unfold
  • Tension may remain high, but direction is set
  • In tragedy: Movement toward catastrophe
  • Loose ends begin to tie up
Example - Romeo and Juliet: Romeo doesn't receive the message explaining the plan, finds Juliet seemingly dead, buys poison, goes to her tomb.

5. D√©nouement/Resolution (Catastrophe in Tragedy)

  • Final outcome is revealed
  • All conflicts are resolved
  • In Tragedy: Death of protagonist(s), order restored at a cost
  • In Comedy: Marriages, reconciliations, happy endings
  • New equilibrium established
Example - Romeo and Juliet: Romeo kills himself, Juliet wakes and kills herself, families discover the bodies and reconcile - but at tragic cost.

Three-Act Structure (Modern Drama)

‚ñº

Modern dramas and films often use a simplified three-act structure:

Act 1 - Setup:

  • Introduces characters, setting, and situation
  • Establishes protagonist's goal or desire
  • Ends with an inciting incident that propels story forward
  • Approximately 25% of the story

Act 2 - Confrontation:

  • Protagonist pursues goal but faces obstacles
  • Conflicts escalate
  • Character development occurs
  • Midpoint: Major revelation or setback
  • Approximately 50% of the story

Act 3 - Resolution:

  • Climax occurs
  • Final confrontation
  • All questions answered
  • New status quo established
  • Approximately 25% of the story

Key Structural Elements

‚ñº

Plot vs. Story:

  • Story: Chronological sequence of all events
  • Plot: Artistic arrangement of events for dramatic effect
  • Plot may use flashbacks, flash-forwards, or non-linear structure

Subplot:

  • Secondary storyline running parallel to main plot
  • Often involves supporting characters
  • Functions: Provides contrast, reinforces theme, adds complexity
  • Should connect to or comment on main plot

Dramatic Irony:

  • Audience knows something characters don't
  • Creates tension and suspense
  • Particularly effective in tragedy
Example: In Romeo and Juliet, we know Juliet is only sleeping, but Romeo doesn't, making his suicide even more tragic.

Foreshadowing:

  • Hints or clues about future events
  • Creates anticipation and unity
  • Can be subtle or obvious

Unity of Action, Time, and Place (Classical Unities):

  • Action: Single main plot without subplots
  • Time: Events occur within 24 hours
  • Place: Single location
  • Not all plays follow these rules (Shakespeare often ignored them)

Analyzing Dramatic Structure

‚ñº

Questions to Ask:

  • How is the play structured? (Five acts, three acts, other?)
  • Where is the exposition? What information is provided?
  • What is the inciting incident?
  • How does tension build in the rising action?
  • Where is the climax? Why is it the turning point?
  • How do events in falling action lead to the resolution?
  • Is the resolution satisfying? Why or why not?
  • Are there subplots? How do they relate to the main plot?
  • Does the playwright use foreshadowing? Where and how?
  • Is the structure linear or does it use flashbacks?

Useful Phrases for Analysis:

  • "The exposition establishes..."
  • "The inciting incident occurs when..."
  • "Tension builds as..."
  • "The climax represents a turning point because..."
  • "The structure follows/deviates from classical conventions by..."
  • "The playwright uses foreshadowing to..."
  • "The resolution brings closure to... but leaves... unresolved"
  • "The subplot mirrors/contrasts with the main plot by..."
Analysis Tip: Not all plays follow Freytag's pyramid exactly. Modern drama often experiments with structure. Analyze how the actual structure serves the play's themes and effects.

Tragedy vs. Comedy Structure

‚ñº

Tragedy:

  • Tragic Hero: Noble character with fatal flaw (hamartia)
  • Peripeteia: Reversal of fortune from good to bad
  • Anagnorisis: Moment of recognition/discovery
  • Catharsis: Emotional purging for audience (pity and fear)
  • Structure: Rising to peak, then catastrophic fall
  • Ending: Death, suffering, but often restoration of order

Comedy:

  • Begins in confusion or disorder
  • Characters face obstacles (often misunderstandings)
  • Complications create humor
  • Structure: Descending into chaos, then ascending to resolution
  • Ending: Marriages, reconciliations, restored harmony
  • Multiple plotlines often converge at end
Shakespearean Comedy Example (A Midsummer Night's Dream):
  • Beginning: Four lovers in conflicted relationships
  • Middle: Magic creates complete confusion in the forest
  • End: Magic resolved, three marriages, harmony restored

Ending

Types of Endings

‚ñº

1. Closed/Resolved Ending:

  • All major conflicts are resolved
  • Questions answered, loose ends tied up
  • Clear sense of closure
  • Character's fate is certain
  • Effect: Provides satisfaction, completeness
Example: Pride and Prejudice ends with Elizabeth and Darcy's marriage, Lydia and Wickham settled, Jane and Bingley happy - all conflicts resolved.

2. Open/Ambiguous Ending:

  • Some questions left unanswered
  • Character's future uncertain
  • Multiple interpretations possible
  • Reader must draw own conclusions
  • Effect: Thought-provoking, realistic, unsettling
Example: In The Lady or the Tiger?, we never learn whether the door reveals a lady or a tiger - the reader must decide.

3. Circular Ending:

  • Story ends where it began
  • Returns to opening situation or image
  • Suggests cyclical nature of life or unchanging patterns
  • Effect: Emphasizes theme, shows lack of progress or eternal recurrence

4. Surprise/Twist Ending:

  • Unexpected revelation or reversal
  • Forces reader to reconsider entire story
  • Should be surprising yet logical in retrospect
  • Effect: Shock, requires re-evaluation
Example: In The Sixth Sense, the revelation that Dr. Malcolm Crowe is dead reframes the entire story.

5. Tragic Ending:

  • Protagonist dies or suffers greatly
  • Results from character flaw or circumstances
  • Often includes element of inevitability
  • Effect: Catharsis, moral lesson, emotional impact

6. Happy/Comic Ending:

  • Protagonist achieves goals
  • Often includes marriages, reunions
  • Order and harmony restored
  • Effect: Satisfaction, optimism, celebration

7. Bittersweet Ending:

  • Contains both positive and negative elements
  • Victory comes at a cost
  • Success mixed with loss
  • Effect: Complex emotional response, reflects life's complexity
Example: In The Lord of the Rings, evil is defeated, but the Shire is damaged and Frodo is too wounded to remain - victory with sacrifice.

Functions of Endings

‚ñº

1. Providing Resolution:

  • Answers central questions
  • Resolves main conflicts
  • Shows consequences of actions
  • Fulfills or subverts expectations

2. Reinforcing Theme:

  • Final statement of story's meaning
  • Demonstrates moral or lesson
  • Crystallizes central ideas

3. Completing Character Arc:

  • Shows how protagonist has changed
  • Reveals growth or failure to grow
  • Demonstrates consequences of choices

4. Creating Emotional Impact:

  • Evokes specific emotions (joy, sadness, shock)
  • Provides catharsis
  • Leaves lasting impression

5. Prompting Reflection:

  • Especially with open endings
  • Encourages reader to consider implications
  • Raises questions about meaning

Analyzing Endings

‚ñº

Questions to Ask:

  • What type of ending is it? (Open, closed, circular, etc.)
  • Are all conflicts resolved? Which ones, if not?
  • Is the ending surprising or predictable? Is it satisfying?
  • How has the protagonist changed by the end?
  • What happens to major characters?
  • Does the ending reinforce or subvert the story's themes?
  • What is the emotional tone of the ending?
  • Does the ending feel inevitable or arbitrary?
  • Are there any final symbols or images? What do they represent?
  • What is the last line/image? Why might the author choose this?
  • How does the ending reflect on the beginning?

Useful Phrases for Analysis:

  • "The ending provides closure by..."
  • "The ambiguous ending leaves the reader to..."
  • "The resolution suggests that..."
  • "The final scene/image symbolizes..."
  • "The protagonist's fate demonstrates..."
  • "The ending subverts expectations by..."
  • "The bittersweet conclusion reflects..."
  • "By returning to..., the circular ending emphasizes..."
  • "The twist ending forces us to reconsider..."
  • "The ending reinforces the theme of... by showing..."
Analysis Tip: A "good" ending doesn't necessarily mean a happy one. Evaluate whether the ending is appropriate for the story's genre, themes, and tone. Does it feel earned based on what came before?

Famous Controversial Endings

‚ñº

The Great Gatsby:

  • Gatsby dies, dream destroyed, Nick returns west disillusioned
  • Final image: green light and boats against the current
  • Bittersweet and melancholic
  • Reinforces theme: American Dream is illusion

Nineteen Eighty-Four:

  • Winston completely broken, loves Big Brother
  • Dystopian nightmare complete - no hope
  • Devastating and pessimistic
  • Shows totalitarianism's complete victory

Romeo and Juliet:

  • Both lovers dead, families reconcile
  • Tragic but brings social change
  • "For never was a story of more woe..."
  • Theme: Hatred's destructive power

The Merchant of Venice:

  • Officially a comedy, but Shylock's fate is dark
  • Marriages and resolution for Christians, but forced conversion for Shylock
  • Raises questions about justice and mercy
  • Ambiguous moral conclusion

Characterization

What is Characterization?

‚ñº

Characterization is the process by which an author reveals a character's personality, traits, motivations, and development. It's how we come to understand who characters are and why they behave as they do.

Two Types of Characterization:

1. Direct/Explicit Characterization:

  • Author or narrator directly tells us about character
  • Straightforward statements about personality, appearance, background
  • Less subtle but efficient
Example: "Mr. Bennet was so odd a mixture of quick parts, sarcastic humour, reserve, and caprice..." (Pride and Prejudice)

2. Indirect/Implicit Characterization:

  • Character revealed through actions, speech, thoughts, appearance, and others' reactions
  • Reader must infer traits from evidence
  • More subtle and engaging - "show, don't tell"

The STEAL Method (Indirect Characterization):

Element What to Analyze Example Questions
Speech What character says, how they say it Formal or informal? Dialect? Vocabulary level? What do their words reveal?
Thoughts Internal monologue, feelings, opinions What occupies their mind? What do they value? Their self-perception?
Effect on Others How other characters react to them Are they feared, loved, respected? How do others describe them?
Actions What they do, how they behave What choices do they make? How do they treat others? Patterns of behavior?
Looks Physical appearance, clothing, mannerisms What does their appearance suggest? Any symbolic details?

Types of Characters

‚ñº

By Role in Story:

Protagonist:

  • Main character, central to the story
  • Often (but not always) heroic
  • Reader typically sympathizes with protagonist
  • Drives the action forward

Antagonist:

  • Opposes the protagonist
  • Creates conflict and obstacles
  • Not always a person (can be nature, society, internal conflict)
  • Not necessarily "evil" - may have valid motivations
Example: In Othello, Iago is the antagonist who manipulates Othello (protagonist) through lies and deception.

Supporting Characters:

  • Foil: Character who contrasts with another (usually protagonist) to highlight specific traits
  • Confidant: Character protagonist confides in, revealing inner thoughts
  • Love Interest: Romantic partner or potential partner
  • Mentor: Guides or teaches protagonist
Foil Example: In Romeo and Juliet, Mercutio (witty, cynical about love) acts as a foil to Romeo (romantic, idealistic).

By Complexity:

Round Characters:

  • Complex, multidimensional personalities
  • Have contradictory traits (like real people)
  • Possess depth and realistic psychology
  • Capable of surprising us in believable ways
  • Usually protagonists are round characters
Example: Hamlet - intellectual yet impulsive, loving yet cruel, decisive yet procrastinating. His complexity makes him fascinating.

Flat Characters:

  • One-dimensional, defined by single trait or quality
  • Predictable and uncomplicated
  • Serve specific function in plot
  • Often minor characters or comic relief
  • Not necessarily "bad" - serve their purpose

By Development:

Dynamic Characters:

  • Undergo significant internal change throughout story
  • Learn from experiences
  • Transform in beliefs, personality, or understanding
  • Change should be credible and motivated
  • Usually protagonists in coming-of-age stories
Example: Elizabeth Bennet (Pride and Prejudice) changes from prejudiced against Darcy to understanding and loving him, learning to overcome her pride.

Static Characters:

  • Remain essentially the same throughout
  • Don't undergo significant transformation
  • Provide stability or contrast to dynamic characters
  • Can still be complex (round) characters
Important: Static doesn't mean boring or poorly written. Sherlock Holmes is static but fascinating. The key is whether change is appropriate for the story's purpose.

By Realism:

Realistic Characters:

  • Behave in psychologically believable ways
  • Have mixed motivations
  • Complex moral nature

Symbolic/Allegorical Characters:

  • Represent abstract concepts or ideas
  • Common in allegories, fables, morality plays
  • Function more as symbols than realistic people

Stock Characters/Archetypes:

  • Conventional, stereotypical figures
  • Immediately recognizable types
  • Examples: wicked stepmother, wise old man, innocent maiden
  • Can be effective if used deliberately

Character Development and Arc

‚ñº

A character arc is the transformation or inner journey of a character throughout the narrative.

Positive/Growth Arc:

  • Character overcomes flaws
  • Learns valuable lesson
  • Becomes better person
  • Common in coming-of-age stories
Example: In A Christmas Carol, Scrooge transforms from miserly and cold to generous and warm-hearted.

Negative/Fall Arc:

  • Character degenerates morally
  • Often due to fatal flaw (hamartia)
  • Downward spiral to destruction
  • Common in tragedies
Example: In Macbeth, ambition transforms Macbeth from noble warrior to murderous tyrant.

Flat Arc:

  • Character doesn't change but changes the world around them
  • Already has truth/conviction at story's start
  • Tests and challenges their beliefs but they hold firm
  • Their steadfastness transforms others

Stages of Character Development:

  1. Characterization: Who is the character at story's beginning?
  2. Want/Goal: What does the character desire?
  3. Need: What does the character actually require (often unconscious)?
  4. Lie/Misbelief: What false belief holds them back?
  5. Obstacles: What challenges do they face?
  6. Choice/Climax: What crucial decision do they make?
  7. Transformation: How have they changed?
  8. Resolution: Who are they at story's end?

Motivation and Psychology

‚ñº

Understanding Character Motivation:

Motivation explains why characters act as they do.

Types of Motivation:

  • External: Driven by outside forces (survival, wealth, love, revenge)
  • Internal: Driven by emotions, beliefs, values, psychological needs
  • Conscious: Character aware of their reasons
  • Unconscious: Hidden desires, repressed emotions, unrecognized needs

Maslow's Hierarchy (Understanding Needs):

  • Physiological: Food, water, shelter, survival
  • Safety: Security, stability, protection
  • Love/Belonging: Relationships, acceptance, connection
  • Esteem: Respect, recognition, status, achievement
  • Self-Actualization: Fulfilling potential, meaning, purpose
Example: In The Hunger Games, Katniss's initial motivation is physiological (survival) and safety, but evolves to include belonging (protecting Prim, connecting with Peeta) and eventually self-actualization (becoming symbol of rebellion).

Internal vs. External Conflict:

Internal Conflict:

  • Psychological struggle within character
  • Competing desires, moral dilemmas, self-doubt
  • Often more complex and interesting

External Conflict:

  • Character vs. Character
  • Character vs. Society
  • Character vs. Nature
  • Character vs. Fate/Supernatural
  • Character vs. Technology
Best Stories: Often combine external conflict (plot) with internal conflict (character development). External obstacles force characters to confront internal issues.

Analyzing Characterization

‚ñº

Essential Questions:

  • What type of character is this? (Round/flat, dynamic/static, protagonist/antagonist?)
  • How is the character revealed? (Direct or indirect characterization?)
  • What are their defining traits?
  • What motivates them? What do they want vs. need?
  • What conflicts do they face (internal and external)?
  • How do they change throughout the story?
  • What do they represent thematically?
  • How do other characters view them?
  • What are their strengths and weaknesses?
  • Are they believable/realistic?

Character Analysis Examples

‚ñº

Example 1: Lady Macbeth

  • Type: Round, dynamic character; antagonist (though Macbeth is protagonist)
  • Initial Traits: Ambitious, manipulative, strong-willed, ruthless
  • Motivation: Power, status for husband, escaping feminine weakness
  • Character Arc: Negative - descends from confident manipulator to guilt-ridden suicide
  • Key Moment: "Out, damned spot!" - guilt manifests as hallucination
  • Symbolism: Represents unchecked ambition's destructive power
  • Complexity: Initially appears stronger than Macbeth but ultimately more vulnerable

Example 2: Atticus Finch (To Kill a Mockingbird)

  • Type: Round, static character; protagonist (though Scout narrates)
  • Traits: Principled, compassionate, wise, courageous, patient
  • Motivation: Justice, protecting innocents, teaching children morality
  • Character Arc: Flat - maintains moral compass, transforms community instead
  • Function: Moral center, mentor figure, represents ideal of integrity
  • Characterization: Through actions (defending Tom Robinson), speech (courtroom eloquence), children's admiration

Example 3: Holden Caulfield (The Catcher in the Rye)

  • Type: Round, dynamic character; protagonist and narrator
  • Initial State: Alienated, cynical, depressed, rebellious teenager
  • Motivation: Protect childhood innocence (especially sister Phoebe), resist "phoniness" of adult world
  • Internal Conflict: Desire to preserve innocence vs. reality of maturation
  • Characterization: First-person narration reveals his thoughts directly; distinctive voice (slang, repetition)
  • Development: Gradual realization he cannot stop change; mental breakdown leads to uncertain recovery

Stylistic Devices

What are Stylistic Devices?

‚ñº

Stylistic devices (also called literary devices, rhetorical devices, or figures of speech) are techniques writers use to create special effects, convey meaning, and make their writing more engaging and memorable.

Purpose of Stylistic Devices:

  • Create vivid imagery
  • Emphasize important ideas
  • Evoke emotions
  • Add layers of meaning
  • Make language memorable
  • Create rhythm and sound patterns
  • Establish tone and mood
  • Enhance persuasiveness
Analysis Warning: Don't just identify devices - explain their effect and function. How do they contribute to meaning, tone, or theme?

Sound Devices

‚ñº

Alliteration:

Definition: Repetition of initial consonant sounds in neighboring words

  • Effect: Creates musicality, emphasizes words, makes phrases memorable
  • Example: "Peter Piper picked a peck of pickled peppers"
  • Literary Example: "From forth the fatal foes of these two foes" (Romeo and Juliet)

Assonance:

Definition: Repetition of vowel sounds within words

  • Effect: Creates internal rhyme, musicality, mood
  • Example: "The rain in Spain stays mainly in the plain"
  • Literary Example: "Hear the mellow wedding bells" (Poe) - repetition of 'e' sound

Consonance:

Definition: Repetition of consonant sounds within or at the end of words

  • Effect: Creates harmony, links words thematically
  • Example: "All mammals named Sam are clammy" (repetition of 'm')

Onomatopoeia:

Definition: Words that imitate sounds they describe

  • Effect: Creates vivid sensory experience, brings action to life
  • Examples: buzz, hiss, crash, whisper, bang, sizzle, murmur
  • Literary Example: "The murmurous haunt of flies on summer eves" (Keats)

Rhythm and Meter:

Definition: Pattern of stressed and unstressed syllables in poetry

  • Iambic Pentameter: Most common in English poetry (Shakespeare's favorite)
  • Effect: Creates musical quality, affects pace and mood
  • Example: "Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?" (da-DUM da-DUM pattern)

Rhyme:

  • End Rhyme: Words at end of lines rhyme
  • Internal Rhyme: Rhyme within a line
  • Effect: Creates pattern, emphasizes connections, aids memory

Imagery and Figurative Language

‚ñº

Imagery:

Definition: Descriptive language appealing to the five senses

  • Visual: What we see (colors, shapes, movement)
  • Auditory: What we hear (sounds, music, voices)
  • Tactile: What we touch (textures, temperatures)
  • Olfactory: What we smell
  • Gustatory: What we taste
  • Effect: Makes writing vivid, concrete, engaging; creates atmosphere
Example: "It was a cold, grey day in late November. The weather had changed overnight, when a backing wind brought a granite sky and a mizzling rain with it." - Multiple sensory details create bleak atmosphere.

Metaphor:

Definition: Direct comparison stating one thing IS another (not literally true)

  • Effect: Creates new understanding, reveals similarities, compresses meaning
  • Example: "Time is money" / "The world is a stage"
  • Literary Example: "Juliet is the sun" (Romeo and Juliet) - suggests warmth, light, life-giving qualities

Simile:

Definition: Comparison using "like" or "as"

  • Effect: Similar to metaphor but more explicit comparison
  • Example: "My love is like a red, red rose"
  • Literary Example: "I wandered lonely as a cloud" (Wordsworth)

Personification:

Definition: Giving human qualities to non-human things

  • Effect: Makes abstract concrete, creates emotional connection
  • Example: "The wind whispered through the trees"
  • Literary Example: "Death, be not proud" (Donne) - addresses death as person

Hyperbole:

Definition: Extreme exaggeration for emphasis

  • Effect: Creates humor, emphasizes point, shows intensity of feeling
  • Example: "I've told you a million times"
  • Literary Example: "I loved Ophelia: forty thousand brothers / Could not, with all their quantity of love, / Make up my sum" (Hamlet)

Understatement/Litotes:

Definition: Deliberate downplaying for effect (litotes uses double negative)

  • Effect: Creates irony, humor, or emphasis through restraint
  • Example: Calling a tornado "a bit of wind" / "not bad" meaning "very good"

Oxymoron:

Definition: Combination of contradictory terms

  • Effect: Creates paradox, highlights complexity, provokes thought
  • Examples: "deafening silence," "bittersweet," "living death"
  • Literary Example: "Parting is such sweet sorrow" (Romeo and Juliet)

Paradox:

Definition: Statement that seems contradictory but reveals truth

  • Effect: Challenges thinking, reveals deeper meaning
  • Example: "Less is more" / "The only constant is change"
  • Literary Example: "Fair is foul, and foul is fair" (Macbeth)

Structural and Rhetorical Devices

‚ñº

Repetition:

Definition: Repeating words, phrases, or structures for emphasis

  • Effect: Emphasizes ideas, creates rhythm, aids memory, builds intensity
  • Example: "And the rockets' red glare, the bombs bursting in air..."

Anaphora:

Definition: Repetition of word/phrase at beginning of successive clauses

  • Effect: Creates rhythm, emphasizes ideas, builds momentum
  • Example: "I have a dream... I have a dream... I have a dream..." (Martin Luther King Jr.)

Epistrophe:

Definition: Repetition at end of successive clauses

  • Effect: Similar to anaphora, emphasizes ending point
  • Example: "When I was a child, I spoke as a child, I understood as a child, I thought as a child"

Parallelism:

Definition: Similar grammatical structure in successive phrases

  • Effect: Creates balance, rhythm, emphasizes relationships between ideas
  • Example: "Easy come, easy go" / "Ask not what your country can do for you‚Äîask what you can do for your country"

Antithesis:

Definition: Contrasting ideas in parallel structure

  • Effect: Emphasizes contrast, creates memorable phrases
  • Example: "It was the best of times, it was the worst of times" (Dickens)
  • Example: "That's one small step for man, one giant leap for mankind"

Rhetorical Question:

Definition: Question asked for effect, not expecting answer

  • Effect: Engages reader, emphasizes point, implies obvious answer
  • Example: "If you prick us, do we not bleed?" (The Merchant of Venice)
  • Example: "Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?" (Shakespeare)

Climax (Gradation):

Definition: Arrangement of words/ideas in order of increasing importance

  • Effect: Builds tension, creates dramatic effect
  • Example: "I came, I saw, I conquered" (Caesar)

Anticlimax:

Definition: Sudden shift from serious to trivial

  • Effect: Creates humor, deflates tension, can be satirical
  • Example: "He lost his family, his car, and his cell phone"

Symbolism and Extended Devices

‚ñº

Symbol:

Definition: Object, person, or action representing abstract idea

  • Effect: Adds depth, allows multiple interpretations, universal meaning
  • Types:
    • Universal: Widely recognized (dove = peace, darkness = evil)
    • Contextual: Specific to work (green light in Gatsby = American Dream)
  • Examples: Cross (Christianity), red rose (love), journey (life), chains (oppression)

Allegory:

Definition: Extended metaphor where entire story represents something else

  • Effect: Teaches moral lesson, comments on society, allows critique
  • Example: Animal Farm (Orwell) - animals represent figures in Russian Revolution
  • Example: The Crucible (Miller) - witch trials represent McCarthy era

Motif:

Definition: Recurring element (image, symbol, idea) throughout work

  • Effect: Reinforces themes, creates unity, builds meaning through repetition
  • Example: Blood in Macbeth (guilt, violence), light/dark in Romeo and Juliet

Allusion:

Definition: Reference to well-known person, event, place, or work

  • Effect: Adds depth, creates connections, shows erudition
  • Types: Biblical, mythological, historical, literary
  • Example: "He was a real Romeo with the ladies" (allusion to Shakespeare)
  • Example: "This place is like a Garden of Eden" (Biblical allusion)

Foreshadowing:

Definition: Hints or clues about future events

  • Effect: Creates suspense, prepares reader, adds unity
  • Example: Romeo's premonition: "My mind misgives / Some consequence yet hanging in the stars"

Irony:

Definition: Contrast between expectation and reality

  • Verbal Irony: Saying opposite of what's meant (sarcasm is harsh form)
    • Example: "Nice weather!" during a storm
  • Situational Irony: Outcome opposite of expectation
    • Example: Fire station burns down
    • Literary Example: Romeo kills himself believing Juliet dead; she wakes moments later
  • Dramatic Irony: Audience knows something characters don't
    • Example: In Othello, audience knows Iago is lying but Othello doesn't

Analyzing Stylistic Devices

‚ñº

Three-Step Analysis Process:

  1. IDENTIFY: Name the device and quote the example
  2. EXPLAIN: Describe how it works in this specific context
  3. ANALYZE EFFECT: Explain WHY the author uses it - what effect does it have?
Example Analysis:

Quote: "All the world's a stage, / And all the men and women merely players" (As You Like It)

Device: Extended metaphor

Explanation: Shakespeare compares life to a theatrical performance, with people as actors.

Effect: This metaphor suggests life is temporary and performative. It emphasizes the idea that people play different roles at different life stages, and implies a lack of authentic identity. The word "merely" suggests people have limited control over their lives, following a predetermined script. This reinforces the speech's melancholic tone about life's transience and meaninglessness.

Questions to Guide Analysis:

  • What is the author drawing attention to with this device?
  • How does it affect the tone or mood?
  • Does it relate to the themes?
  • What emotions does it evoke?
  • Does it reveal character or advance plot?
  • Why might the author choose this device over others?
  • How does it connect to other devices or patterns in the text?

Common Phrases for Analysis:

  • "The author employs [device] to..."
  • "Through the use of [device], the writer..."
  • "This [device] emphasizes/highlights/reinforces..."
  • "The effect of this [device] is..."
  • "By using [device], the author creates/evokes/suggests..."
  • "The [device] contributes to the [tone/mood/theme] by..."
  • "This technique serves to..."
  • "The repetition of [x] throughout the passage reinforces..."
Common Mistake: "The author uses a metaphor to make it more interesting." This is too vague! Always explain the specific effect of the device in this particular context.

Practice Approach:

  • Read passage multiple times
  • Highlight/mark devices you notice
  • Look for patterns (repeated devices or related imagery)
  • Consider how devices work together
  • Connect devices to theme, character, or tone
  • Select most significant devices for detailed analysis
  • Avoid simply listing devices - integrate analysis into discussion

Narrative Perspective

What is Narrative Perspective?

‚ñº

Narrative perspective (also called point of view or POV) refers to the position from which a story is told. It determines what information readers receive and how they experience the story.

Why Narrative Perspective Matters:

  • Controls what information readers know
  • Influences reader's emotional connection to characters
  • Affects reliability of information
  • Shapes tone and style of narrative
  • Determines level of intimacy or distance
  • Can create suspense, mystery, or dramatic irony
Important Distinction: Narrator ‚â† Author. The narrator is the voice telling the story (may be a character or separate entity). The author is the real person who wrote the work.

First-Person Narrative (I/We)

‚ñº

Definition:

Story told by a character within the story using "I" or "we".

Characteristics:

  • Narrator is participant in events
  • Direct access to narrator's thoughts and feelings
  • Limited to narrator's knowledge and perspective
  • Can only report what narrator witnesses or is told
  • Uses first-person pronouns: I, me, my, we, us, our

Types of First-Person Narrators:

1. First-Person Central (Protagonist):

  • Narrator is the main character
  • Story is about narrator's experiences
  • Most intimate perspective
  • Example: "The Catcher in the Rye" (Holden Caulfield narrates his own story)

2. First-Person Peripheral (Witness):

  • Narrator is not main character but observes events
  • Story focuses on another character
  • Provides outside perspective on protagonist
  • Example: "The Great Gatsby" (Nick Carraway narrates Gatsby's story)

Advantages of First-Person:

  • Intimacy: Direct access to narrator's inner world
  • Authenticity: Feels personal and immediate
  • Voice: Strong, distinctive narrative voice possible
  • Empathy: Reader identifies closely with narrator
  • Suspense: Limited knowledge creates mystery

Disadvantages/Limitations:

  • Limited Scope: Can't show what narrator doesn't witness
  • Bias: Everything filtered through one perspective
  • Reliability: Narrator may be mistaken or dishonest
  • Multiple Settings: Difficult to show simultaneous events
Example - Opening of Moby Dick:

"Call me Ishmael. Some years ago‚Äînever mind how long precisely‚Äîhaving little or no money in my purse, and nothing particular to interest me on shore, I thought I would sail about a little and see the watery part of the world."

Analysis: Immediately personal and conversational. We're in Ishmael's head, experiencing his thoughts and decisions directly. His casual tone ("never mind how long precisely") creates intimacy.

Unreliable Narrator:

A first-person narrator whose credibility is compromised.

  • Reasons for Unreliability:
    • Mental illness or delusion
    • Young age/naivety
    • Deliberate deception
    • Limited understanding
    • Extreme bias or prejudice
  • Effect: Creates irony, suspense; reader must question and interpret
  • Example: "The Tell-Tale Heart" (Poe) - narrator insists he's sane while describing murder driven by paranoia

Third-Person Narrative (He/She/They)

‚ñº

Definition:

Story told by narrator outside the story using third-person pronouns: he, she, they.

Third-Person Omniscient:

Definition: All-knowing narrator with unlimited access to all characters' thoughts, feelings, and events.

  • Characteristics:
    • Can enter any character's mind
    • Knows past, present, and future
    • Can be in multiple places simultaneously
    • May comment on events and characters
    • God-like perspective
  • Advantages:
    • Complete freedom to show any event
    • Can reveal all characters' motivations
    • Can provide context and commentary
    • Shows multiple perspectives
  • Disadvantages:
    • Less intimate than first-person
    • Can feel distant or detached
    • Risk of "head-hopping" confusion
    • Less suspense (narrator knows everything)
Example - Pride and Prejudice:

"Mr. Bennet was so odd a mixture of quick parts, sarcastic humour, reserve, and caprice, that the experience of three-and-twenty years had been insufficient to make his wife understand his character. Her mind was less difficult to develop. She was a woman of mean understanding, little information, and uncertain temper."

Analysis: Narrator knows both characters intimately, reveals their inner natures, and makes judgments about them. This omniscient view allows Austen to show both sides of the marriage.

Third-Person Limited:

Definition: Narrator focuses on one character's perspective, accessing only that character's thoughts and feelings.

  • Characteristics:
    • Follows one character closely
    • Shows only what that character knows/experiences
    • Can describe character from outside
    • Still uses "he/she/they" not "I"
    • May shift focus between chapters
  • Advantages:
    • Combines intimacy of first-person with flexibility of third
    • Can describe focal character objectively
    • Creates mystery about other characters
    • Multiple limited perspectives possible (different chapters)
Example - Harry Potter:

"Harry had never believed he would meet a boy he hated more than Dudley, but that was before he met Draco Malfoy."

Analysis: Third-person but limited to Harry's perspective. We know Harry's feelings but experience story through third-person narration. We can't know what Draco thinks.

Third-Person Objective (Dramatic):

Definition: Camera-like narrator that reports only observable actions and dialogue, no internal thoughts.

  • Characteristics:
    • No access to any thoughts or feelings
    • Shows only external actions and speech
    • Readers must infer motivations
    • Like watching a film
  • Advantages:
    • Creates objectivity and distance
    • Allows reader to interpret freely
    • Can create mystery and ambiguity
    • Feels realistic and immediate
  • Example: Hemingway's "Hills Like White Elephants" - entirely dialogue and action, readers must infer the unstated topic (abortion)

Second-Person Narrative (You)

‚ñº

Definition:

Rare perspective addressing reader as "you," making reader the protagonist.

Characteristics:

  • Uses "you" throughout narrative
  • Places reader in story as character
  • Can feel intimate or alienating
  • Experimental and unusual

Usage:

  • Instructions and directions
  • "Choose Your Own Adventure" books
  • Some experimental literary fiction
  • Creates immediacy and involvement
Example - Bright Lights, Big City (Jay McInerney):

"You are not the kind of guy who would be at a place like this at this time of the morning. But here you are, and you cannot say that the terrain is entirely unfamiliar..."

Stream of Consciousness

‚ñº

Definition:

Narrative technique presenting character's thoughts as continuous, unfiltered flow, mimicking actual thought processes.

Characteristics:

  • Jumps between topics without transitions
  • May lack conventional grammar/punctuation
  • Includes sensory impressions, memories, associations
  • Reveals subconscious and conscious thought
  • Can be difficult to follow
  • Associated with Modernist writers (Joyce, Woolf, Faulkner)

Techniques:

  • Interior Monologue: Character's thoughts presented directly
  • Free Association: One thought triggers another randomly
  • Sensory Impressions: Raw perception before interpretation
Example - Mrs. Dalloway (Virginia Woolf):

"What a lark! What a plunge! For so it had always seemed to her, when, with a little squeak of the hinges, which she could hear now, she had burst open the French windows and plunged at Bourton into the open air."

Analysis: Thought flows naturally from present moment to memory, capturing how mind actually works with associations and sensory details.

Multiple Perspectives

‚ñº

Definition:

Story told from multiple characters' viewpoints, often in different chapters or sections.

Types:

  • Alternating First-Person: Different characters narrate different chapters in first person
  • Multiple Third-Person Limited: Third-person narrative shifts focus between characters
  • Ensemble Cast: Many equally important perspectives

Advantages:

  • Shows different sides of story/conflict
  • Creates dramatic irony (readers know more than individual characters)
  • Allows exploration of multiple themes/experiences
  • Can show complexity of situations

Examples:

  • The Sound and the Fury (Faulkner): Four different narrators tell same story
  • A Song of Ice and Fire (Martin): Chapters from different characters' viewpoints
  • As I Lay Dying (Faulkner): 15 different narrators

Analyzing Narrative Perspective

‚ñº

Key Questions to Ask:

  • What is the narrative perspective? (First, second, third person?)
  • If third person: omniscient, limited, or objective?
  • Who is the narrator? Character or external voice?
  • Is the narrator reliable or unreliable?
  • What can the narrator know? What are their limitations?
  • How does perspective affect what we learn?
  • What is the narrator's tone/attitude?
  • Does the perspective create sympathy for certain characters?
  • Does perspective shift? When and why?
  • How would the story change with different perspective?

Evidence to Look For:

Perspective Type Pronouns Used Key Indicators
First-Person I, me, my, we, us Character tells own story; "I thought," "I felt"
Second-Person You, your "You walk down the street," addressing reader directly
Third Omniscient He, she, they Multiple characters' thoughts; narrator comments; knows future
Third Limited He, she, they Focus on one character; only that character's thoughts revealed
Third Objective He, she, they No thoughts shown; only actions and dialogue; camera-like

Analyzing the Effect of Perspective:

  • On Information: What does perspective reveal/conceal?
  • On Sympathy: Who do we sympathize with? Why?
  • On Tone: How does narrator's voice affect tone?
  • On Theme: How does perspective relate to themes?
  • On Suspense: Does limited knowledge create mystery?

Useful Phrases for Analysis:

  • "The first-person perspective creates intimacy by..."
  • "The omniscient narrator's ability to... allows the author to..."
  • "The limited perspective means we only learn..."
  • "By restricting the narrative to X's viewpoint, the author..."
  • "The unreliable narrator creates irony because..."
  • "The shift in perspective from X to Y reveals..."
  • "The objective narration forces the reader to..."
  • "The choice of perspective is significant because..."
Critical Thinking: Always ask WHY the author chose this perspective. How would the story be different with another perspective? What is gained or lost?

Perspective in Drama

‚ñº

Special Considerations for Drama:

Plays don't have traditional narrators but still have "perspective" through:

  • Stage Directions: Playwright's voice giving information
  • Soliloquy: Character speaks thoughts aloud (audience hears, other characters don't)
    • Functions like first-person narration
    • Reveals internal state directly
    • Example: Hamlet's "To be or not to be"
  • Aside: Brief comment to audience, unheard by other characters
    • Creates dramatic irony
    • Reveals true thoughts/intentions
    • Example: Iago's asides in Othello
  • Chorus: Group character commenting on action (especially Greek drama)
    • Provides context and interpretation
    • Represents community perspective
  • Dramatic Irony: Audience knows more than characters
    • Creates tension and anticipation
    • Functions like omniscient narration
Romeo and Juliet - Dramatic Irony:

We know Juliet is alive but sleeping. Romeo doesn't know this. This creates devastating dramatic irony as we watch him poison himself.

Comparing Perspectives: Same Story, Different View

‚ñº

Example: Little Red Riding Hood

First-Person (Little Red):

"I walked through the dark forest to Grandmother's house. Something felt wrong, but I couldn't place what it was. When I arrived, Grandmother looked... different somehow."

Effect: Suspenseful, reader shares Little Red's confusion and fear

Third-Person Omniscient:

"Little Red walked innocently through the forest, unaware that the wolf had already devoured her grandmother and was waiting in disguise. The wolf grinned with anticipation."

Effect: Dramatic irony, reader knows more than character, creates tension

Third-Person Limited (Wolf's POV):

"He heard her footsteps approaching. Perfect. She suspected nothing. He adjusted the nightcap and tried to make his voice sound gentle."

Effect: Creates sympathy or understanding for antagonist, shows his perspective

Third-Person Objective:

"The girl knocked on the door. 'Come in,' said a voice from inside. She opened the door and walked to the bed."

Effect: Detached, reader must interpret, ambiguous
