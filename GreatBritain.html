Anglo-Irish Relations (until 1921)

‚ñº

English Conquest and Colonization:

  • 12th Century: English invasion of Ireland began
  • 16th-17th Centuries: Plantation of Ireland - English/Scottish Protestant settlers given confiscated Catholic land
  • Ulster Plantation: Northern Ireland heavily settled by Protestants
  • Penal Laws (1691): Discriminated against Catholics - couldn't vote, own land, hold office

Resistance and Rebellion:

  • 1798 Rebellion: United Irishmen uprising brutally suppressed
  • Act of Union (1800): Ireland formally part of United Kingdom
  • Catholic Emancipation (1829): Some rights restored
  • Great Famine (1845-1852): 1 million died, 1 million emigrated; British response inadequate

Home Rule Movement:

  • Campaign for Irish self-government within UK
  • Three Home Rule Bills (1886, 1893, 1914)
  • Ulster Unionists: Protestant majority in Northern Ireland opposed Home Rule
  • Threat of civil war by 1914

Easter Rising (1916):

  • Armed rebellion in Dublin
  • Declared Irish Republic
  • Brutally suppressed by British
  • Execution of leaders turned them into martyrs
  • Shifted Irish opinion toward complete independence

War of Independence (1919-1921):

  • Sinn F√©in: Won 1918 election, declared independence
  • IRA (Irish Republican Army): Guerrilla war against British
  • Black and Tans: British paramilitaries, notorious for brutality
  • Anglo-Irish Treaty (1921): Ireland partitioned

Partition (1921):

  • Irish Free State: 26 counties, Catholic majority, independent (later Republic of Ireland)
  • Northern Ireland: 6 counties, Protestant majority, remained in UK
  • Border: Gerrymandered to maximize Protestant/Unionist control
  • Legacy: Created lasting sectarian division

The Troubles (1968-1998)

‚ñº

Background:

The Troubles was a 30-year conflict in Northern Ireland between:

  • Nationalists/Republicans: Mostly Catholic, wanted united Ireland
  • Unionists/Loyalists: Mostly Protestant, wanted to remain in UK

Causes:

  • Discrimination: Catholics faced systematic discrimination in housing, employment, voting
  • Sectarianism: Deep religious and cultural divisions
  • Partition: Legacy of 1921 division
  • National Identity: Conflicting allegiances (British vs. Irish)
  • Gerrymandering: Electoral boundaries favored Protestants

Key Events:

1968-69: Civil Rights Movement marches, police violence
1969: British troops deployed to "restore order"
1971: Internment without trial introduced
1972: Bloody Sunday - British soldiers killed 14 unarmed civilians
1981: IRA hunger strikes - Bobby Sands died
1984: Brighton bombing - IRA nearly killed PM Thatcher
1998: Good Friday Agreement - peace settlement

Main Paramilitary Groups:

Republican (Irish Nationalist):

  • IRA (Provisional IRA): Main republican paramilitary
  • Bombings, assassinations, attacks on British forces
  • Political wing: Sinn F√©in

Loyalist (British Unionist):

  • UVF, UDA: Protestant paramilitaries
  • Targeted Catholic civilians
  • Sometimes colluded with British security forces

British Role:

  • British Army presence throughout
  • Special powers - detention, searches
  • Shoot-to-Kill Policy: Controversial tactics
  • Collusion: Evidence of cooperation with loyalist paramilitaries
  • Propaganda War: Censorship, media restrictions

Casualties:

  • 3,500+ killed
  • 47,000+ injured
  • Majority were civilians
  • Psychological trauma across Northern Ireland

Good Friday Agreement (1998):

Key Provisions:

  • Power-Sharing: Mandatory coalition government
  • North-South Bodies: Cooperation with Republic of Ireland
  • Decommissioning: Paramilitaries give up weapons
  • Prisoners: Early release for paramilitary prisoners
  • Policing: Reform of Royal Ulster Constabulary
  • Self-Determination: Northern Ireland's status by consent

Approval:

  • Referendum in Northern Ireland: 71% Yes
  • Referendum in Republic of Ireland: 94% Yes

Post-Good Friday Agreement:

  • Success: Violence dramatically reduced
  • Challenges: Assembly suspended multiple times
  • Dissidents: Some reject peace process
  • Segregation: Communities still divided
  • Brexit Impact: Threatened peace by creating border issues
Legacy: The Troubles represent one of the darkest chapters in modern British history. While the peace process has been largely successful, Northern Ireland remains divided along sectarian lines, with separate schools, housing areas, and ongoing tensions.

16. Britain's Multicultural Society

A Class-ridden Society

‚ñº

British Class System:

Britain has historically been highly stratified by social class, with class consciousness remaining stronger than in many other Western nations.

Traditional Three-Class Model:

  • Upper Class: Aristocracy, landed gentry, old wealth
  • Middle Class: Professionals, business owners, educated
  • Working Class: Manual laborers, factory workers

Modern Class Indicators:

  • Accent: "Received Pronunciation" vs. regional accents
  • Education: Public schools (actually private) vs. state schools
  • Occupation: Professional vs. manual work
  • Cultural Capital: Tastes, manners, consumption patterns
  • Geography: London/South vs. North, urban vs. rural

Class Mobility:

  • Less social mobility than commonly believed
  • Education key but also reproduces class
  • Elite universities (Oxford, Cambridge) still dominated by upper/middle class
  • Decline of manufacturing reduced working-class opportunities

Class and Politics:

  • Historically: Working class voted Labour, middle/upper class Conservative
  • Increasingly complex: Some working class now vote Conservative
  • Brexit showed class divisions remain significant

Class Inequality:

  • Wealth gap increased since 1980s
  • Top 1% own more than bottom 50%
  • Life expectancy varies by class
  • Health outcomes strongly correlated with class

Immigration Waves

‚ñº

Post-WWII Immigration:

Windrush Generation (1948-1971):

  • From Caribbean colonies (Jamaica, Barbados, Trinidad)
  • Invited to address labor shortage
  • Named after Empire Windrush ship (1948)
  • Around 500,000 arrived
  • Faced racism, discrimination despite legal status
  • Windrush Scandal (2018): Many wrongly deported or denied rights

South Asian Immigration (1950s-1970s):

  • From India, Pakistan, Bangladesh
  • Also to address labor shortage
  • Later: East African Asians expelled from Uganda, Kenya
  • Concentrated in Midlands, Northern England

Immigration Restrictions:

  • Commonwealth Immigration Act (1962, 1968): Restricted non-white Commonwealth immigration
  • Shifted from open to controlled immigration
  • Racist intent though not explicitly stated

Recent Immigration (1990s-present):

  • EU Freedom of Movement: Large influx from Eastern Europe (Poland, Romania)
  • Asylum Seekers: Middle East, Africa
  • Students: International students, particularly from China, India
  • Brexit: Ended EU freedom of movement (2021)

Current Demographics:

  • 14% of population foreign-born
  • 18% ethnic minority (Census 2021)
  • London: 40%+ ethnic minority
  • Over 300 languages spoken

Structure of Britain's Multi-ethnic Society

‚ñº

Major Ethnic Groups:

  • White British: 82% (declining)
  • Asian/Asian British: 9.3%
    • Indian: 3.1%
    • Pakistani: 2.7%
    • Bangladeshi: 1.0%
    • Chinese: 0.9%
  • Black/Black British: 4.2%
    • African: 2.5%
    • Caribbean: 1.2%
  • Mixed: 2.9%
  • Other: 2.1%

Geographic Distribution:

  • London: Most diverse (40% ethnic minority)
  • Birmingham, Manchester, Leicester: Large Asian populations
  • Rural Areas: Overwhelmingly white
  • Segregation: Some areas highly segregated

Socioeconomic Profile:

Varies by Group:

  • Indian, Chinese: Often high-achieving, professional
  • Pakistani, Bangladeshi: Higher poverty rates
  • Black Caribbean: Face discrimination, lower educational outcomes
  • Black African: Increasingly educated, professional

Integration Patterns:

  • Language: English dominant by second generation
  • Intermarriage: Increasing, especially for younger generations
  • Cultural Retention: Many maintain ethnic traditions
  • British Identity: Most identify as British alongside ethnic identity

Discrimination and Anger

‚ñº

Forms of Discrimination:

  • Employment: Ethnic minority names less likely to get interviews
  • Housing: Discrimination in rentals, mortgages
  • Education: Lower expectations, exclusion rates
  • Criminal Justice: Stop and search disproportionately affects Black people
  • Daily Microaggressions: Subtle racism in everyday interactions

Institutional Racism:

  • Stephen Lawrence Case (1993): Black teenager murdered by racists; police failed to investigate properly
  • Macpherson Report (1999): Found Metropolitan Police "institutionally racist"
  • Defined as: "collective failure of organization to provide appropriate service to people because of color, culture, or ethnic origin"

2011 Riots:

  • Triggered by police shooting of Mark Duggan (Black man) in Tottenham
  • Spread across England
  • Looting, arson, violence
  • Reflected anger about police relations, inequality, opportunity

Persistent Inequalities:

  • Ethnic minorities overrepresented in poverty
  • Underrepresented in leadership positions
  • Higher unemployment rates
  • Pay gaps persist even with same qualifications

London Bomb Attack 2005 & Sleepwalking into Segregation?

‚ñº

7/7 Bombings (July 7, 2005):

  • Four suicide bombers attacked London transport
  • Three Tube trains and one bus bombed
  • 52 killed, 700+ injured
  • Deadliest terrorist attack in UK history

The Bombers:

  • Four young British Muslim men
  • Three from Pakistani backgrounds, born in UK
  • Seemed integrated - worked, had families
  • Radicalized, inspired by Al-Qaeda
  • Motivated by UK foreign policy (Iraq War)

Impact:

  • Shocked nation - "homegrown terrorism"
  • Increased anti-Muslim sentiment
  • Questions about multiculturalism
  • Debate about integration vs. segregation

"Sleepwalking into Segregation":

Trevor Phillips (Head of Commission for Racial Equality) warned in 2005 that Britain was "sleepwalking into segregation"

Evidence of Segregation:

  • Residential: Ethnic enclaves, "white flight"
  • Schools: Many schools overwhelmingly one ethnicity
  • Parallel Lives: Different communities not mixing
  • Self-Segregation: Some minorities prefer living among own group

Cantle Report (2001 - after race riots):

  • Found communities leading "parallel lives"
  • Little interaction between ethnic groups
  • Mutual ignorance and suspicion
  • Recommended community cohesion policies

Multiculturalism Under Attack:

  • Politicians criticized "state multiculturalism"
  • David Cameron: Multiculturalism has "failed"
  • Shift toward "integration" and "British values"
  • Citizenship tests, language requirements

Counter-Arguments:

  • Segregation exaggerated - most areas mixed
  • Young people increasingly integrated
  • Intermarriage rates rising
  • Problems of class and poverty, not just ethnicity

Arranged Marriage in Asian Communities

‚ñº

Cultural Practice:

Arranged marriage is traditional in South Asian cultures (Indian, Pakistani, Bangladeshi). Families introduce potential partners, couple meets and can consent.

Distinction:

  • Arranged Marriage: Families arrange, but couple consents
  • Forced Marriage: No consent, illegal in UK

Reasons for Arranged Marriage:

  • Cultural tradition, family expectations
  • Maintaining cultural/religious identity
  • Family compatibility considered
  • Practical approach to marriage
  • Strong family bonds

Changing Patterns:

  • More choice given to young people
  • "Assisted marriages" - parents suggest, young people decide
  • Later marriages
  • More education before marriage
  • Increasing "love marriages"

Generational Conflict:

  • First generation: Strong adherence to tradition
  • Second generation: Balance between cultures
  • Third generation: More British than Asian
  • Tensions over marriage choice, lifestyle

Forced Marriage:

  • Illegal: Forced Marriage (Civil Protection) Act 2007
  • Estimates: Thousands affected annually
  • Victims: Mostly young women, but also men
  • Consequences: Domestic abuse, mental health issues
  • Support: Forced Marriage Unit helps victims

"Honour-Based" Violence:

  • Crimes committed to "protect family honour"
  • Refusing arranged marriage can trigger
  • Also: dating, dress, Western lifestyle
  • Can include violence, murder ("honour killing")
  • Illegal and condemned by most community leaders
Cultural Sensitivity: It's important to distinguish between arranged marriages (legal and respected cultural practice) and forced marriages (illegal and abusive). Critics of forced marriage must avoid stigmatizing the entire practice of arranged marriage or Asian communities generally.

17. Religion

The Church of England

‚ñº

Origins:

  • Founded by Henry VIII (1534)
  • Split from Roman Catholic Church
  • Immediate cause: Pope refused annulment of marriage
  • Act of Supremacy: Monarch head of Church

Established Church:

The Church of England is the established church - official state church of England.

  • Supreme Governor: The monarch (currently King Charles III)
  • Bishops in Lords: 26 Church of England bishops sit in House of Lords
  • Parliament: Historically had power over Church doctrine

Characteristics:

  • "Via Media": Middle way between Catholicism and Protestantism
  • Retains some Catholic traditions (bishops, liturgy)
  • But Protestant theology
  • Relatively liberal, inclusive approach

Current Status:

  • 25 million baptized members (but low church attendance)
  • Only 1-2% attend weekly services
  • Declining membership and influence
  • Financial challenges - maintaining historic buildings

Controversies:

  • Women Bishops: Approved 2014 (after long debate)
  • Same-Sex Marriage: Church doesn't perform, but allows blessings
  • LGBT Clergy: Ongoing debates
  • Disestablishment: Some call for separation of Church and State

The Free Churches (Dissenters)

‚ñº

Who Are They?

Free Churches or Nonconformists are Protestant denominations separate from the Church of England.

Major Free Churches:

  • Methodists: Founded by John Wesley (18th century)
  • Baptists: Believer's baptism, congregational governance
  • Presbyterians: Scottish origin, reformed theology
  • Congregationalists: Independent local churches
  • Quakers (Society of Friends): Pacifist, egalitarian
  • United Reformed Church: Union of Congregationalists and Presbyterians

Historical Discrimination:

  • Until 19th century, Dissenters faced legal disabilities
  • Couldn't attend Oxford/Cambridge
  • Couldn't hold public office
  • Test Acts excluded them

Social Impact:

  • Strong in working class
  • Supported Labour movement
  • Social reform movements
  • Temperance, anti-slavery

Current Status:

  • Declining like Church of England
  • Together about 10% of population
  • Low regular attendance

Current Religious Issues in Britain

‚ñº

Religious Demographics (2021 Census):

  • Christian: 46.2% (declining from 59.3% in 2011)
  • No Religion: 37.2% (rising rapidly)
  • Muslim: 6.5% (growing)
  • Hindu: 1.7%
  • Sikh: 0.9%
  • Jewish: 0.5%
  • Buddhist: 0.5%

Secularization:

  • Britain increasingly secular
  • Church attendance very low
  • "Cultural Christians" - identify but don't practice
  • Young people especially non-religious
  • Religion declining in public life

Religious Diversity:

  • Immigration brought religious pluralism
  • Mosques, temples, gurdwaras in cities
  • Accommodations: Halal food, prayer rooms
  • Faith schools - Muslims, Jews, Hindus

Islamophobia:

  • Anti-Muslim prejudice increased post-9/11 and 7/7
  • Hate crimes against Muslims
  • Media stereotyping
  • Discrimination in employment
  • "Prevent" strategy criticized for targeting Muslims

Religious Freedom Debates:

  • Religious Symbols: Should hijab be banned in schools?
  • Faith Schools: Segregation concerns
  • Blasphemy: Legal protections?
  • Religious Accommodation: How far to go?

Disestablishment Debate:

  • Should Church of England remain established?
  • Bishops in Lords anachronistic in multi-faith society?
  • Monarch required to be Anglican
  • Most oppose change - "if not broken, don't fix"

18. Social Welfare

The Welfare State

‚ñº

Origins:

The British welfare state was established after WWII based on the Beveridge Report (1942).

Beveridge's "Five Giants":

  • Want: Poverty
  • Disease: Ill health
  • Ignorance: Lack of education
  • Squalor: Poor housing
  • Idleness: Unemployment

Key Principles:

  • "Cradle to Grave": Support from birth to death
  • Universalism: Benefits for all, not just poor
  • State Responsibility: Government ensures minimum living standards
  • Funded by Taxation: Progressive taxation pays for services

Core Programs Established (1945-1951):

  • NHS (1948): Free healthcare
  • National Insurance: Unemployment, sickness, retirement benefits
  • Child Benefit: Payments for families
  • Council Housing: Government-built social housing
  • Free Education: Expanded secondary education

Evolution:

  • Expansion (1945-1979): Welfare state grew
  • Thatcher Era (1979-1990): Retrenchment, means-testing, privatization
  • New Labour (1997-2010): "Third Way" - investment but with conditionality
  • Austerity (2010-present): Severe cuts to welfare

Current Benefits System:

  • Universal Credit: Consolidated benefit replacing six others
  • State Pension: For retirees
  • Jobseeker's Allowance: For unemployed (conditions attached)
  • Housing Benefit: Help with rent
  • Disability Benefits: For disabled people
  • Child Benefit: For families with children

Criticisms:

From the Right:

  • "Dependency culture" - discourages work
  • Too expensive, unsustainable
  • Fraud and abuse

From the Left:

  • Benefits too low to live on
  • Punitive conditionality
  • Cuts hurt vulnerable
  • Universal Credit poorly designed

Challenges:

  • Aging population
  • Rising healthcare costs
  • Automation reducing jobs
  • Inequality increasing

The National Health Service (NHS)

‚ñº

Founding:

  • Established July 5, 1948
  • Health Minister: Aneurin Bevan (Labour)
  • Revolutionary: Free healthcare for all
  • "No society can call itself civilized if a sick person is denied medical aid because of lack of means"

Core Principles:

  • Free at Point of Use: No payment when receiving care
  • Universal: Available to all residents
  • Comprehensive: All medical conditions covered
  • Funded by taxation

Structure:

  • NHS England, Scotland, Wales, Northern Ireland: Separate systems
  • Primary Care: GPs (family doctors), first point of contact
  • Secondary Care: Hospitals, specialists
  • Emergency Care: A&E departments

What's Free vs. Paid:

Free:

  • GP visits
  • Hospital treatment
  • Emergency care
  • Mental health services

Charged (in England):

  • Prescriptions (¬£9.90 per item - exemptions for many)
  • Dental care (subsidized)
  • Eye tests (subsidized)

Public Perception:

  • Beloved institution - "national treasure"
  • Symbol of British values
  • Featured in London 2012 Olympics opening ceremony
  • Regular polls show overwhelming support

Current Challenges:

  • Funding Crisis: Spending hasn't kept pace with demand
  • Staff Shortages: Nurses, doctors leaving profession
  • Wait Times: Months for appointments, treatments
  • Aging Population: More elderly patients with complex needs
  • COVID-19 Impact: Huge backlog, staff burnout
  • Mental Health: Services overwhelmed

Political Debates:

Privatization Concerns:

  • Increased outsourcing to private companies
  • Labour warns of "backdoor privatization"
  • Conservatives deny plans to privatize

Funding:

  • Both parties promise more funding
  • Debate over how much and where from
  • Tax increases politically difficult

Comparison with Other Systems:

  • More cost-effective than US system
  • But worse outcomes than some European countries
  • Lower spending per capita than comparable nations
Cultural Significance: The NHS is more than healthcare - it's a symbol of British social solidarity and egalitarianism. Any politician suggesting major reforms or privatization faces fierce public opposition.

19. Economy

Economic Superpower of the 19th Century

‚ñº

Industrial Revolution:

  • Britain first industrialized nation (late 18th century)
  • "Workshop of the World": Manufacturing powerhouse
  • Innovations: Steam engine, railways, factories
  • Coal and iron resources

Global Dominance (19th Century):

  • Largest economy in world
  • Controlled 25% of world trade
  • Pound sterling global reserve currency
  • London financial center of world
  • "Pax Britannica" - British-dominated world order

Sources of Wealth:

  • Manufacturing: Textiles, steel, shipbuilding
  • Trade: Global commerce through empire
  • Finance: Banking, insurance
  • Imperial Exploitation: Raw materials, captive markets

Decline (1945-1979)

‚ñº

Post-WWII Decline:

  • Exhausted by two world wars
  • Loss of empire
  • Outdated industries
  • Overtaken by US, Germany, Japan

Economic Problems (1945-1979):

  • Low Growth: "British disease" - stagnation
  • Strikes: Labor unrest, "Winter of Discontent" (1978-79)
  • Inflation: Rising prices
  • Unemployment: Increasing joblessness
  • Balance of Payments: Importing more than exporting
  • Nationalization: State ownership of industries often inefficient

"Sick Man of Europe":

  • Britain seen as declining, failing economy
  • Frequent IMF bailouts
  • Power cuts, three-day work week
  • Garbage strikes, bodies unburied

Pre-Brexit Boom

‚ñº

Thatcher Revolution (1979-1990):

  • Privatization: Sold state-owned industries
  • Deregulation: Freed up financial sector
  • Trade Union Reform: Reduced union power
  • "Big Bang" (1986): Deregulated financial markets
  • Free Market: Less government intervention

New Labour (1997-2010):

  • Accepted Thatcherite reforms
  • But increased public spending
  • "Cool Britannia" - cultural renaissance
  • Strong growth, low unemployment
  • City of London booming

Economic Transformation:

  • Deindustrialization: Manufacturing declined
  • Service Economy: Finance, business services, creative industries
  • London-Centric: Capital dominant
  • EU Single Market: Benefited from European integration

2008 Financial Crisis:

  • Banks overleveraged, required bailouts
  • Recession, unemployment rose
  • Austerity policies from 2010
  • But economy recovered by mid-2010s

Structural Changes & A Prosperous South and a Poor North

‚ñº

Deindustrialization:

  • Manufacturing jobs collapsed (1970s-1990s)
  • Coal mines, steel mills, shipyards closed
  • Devastated Northern England, Wales, Scotland
  • Communities built around industry lost purpose

Service Economy:

  • 80% of economy now services
  • Finance: City of London global center
  • Business Services: Consulting, law, accounting
  • Creative Industries: Music, film, TV, advertising
  • Tourism: Major sector

North-South Divide:

South/London:

  • Prosperous, service-based economy
  • High incomes, employment
  • Property values soaring
  • Finance, tech, creative industries

North/Midlands:

  • Struggled after deindustrialization
  • Lower incomes, higher unemployment
  • Cheaper housing but less opportunity
  • Some recovery but lags South

Regional Inequality:

  • Widest regional inequality in developed world
  • London GDP per capita double some Northern regions
  • Life expectancy varies by region
  • Investment concentrated in London

Levelling Up:

  • Boris Johnson pledged to reduce inequality
  • "Levelling up" became government slogan
  • Promise to invest in Northern England
  • Critics say insufficient action

Brexit Impact:

  • Northern England voted heavily for Brexit
  • Economic performance worse post-Brexit
  • Trade barriers with EU
  • Some businesses relocated to EU
  • Labor shortages in some sectors
  • Long-term impact still unfolding

Current Economic Challenges:

  • Low Growth: Anemic compared to peers
  • Productivity: Lower than competitors
  • Inequality: Wealth gap widening
  • Cost of Living Crisis: Inflation, energy costs
  • Public Services: Underfunded after austerity
