Regular verbs follow a predictable pattern: they form the past simple and past participle by adding -ed to the base form.

Pronunciation of -ed Endings

The -ed ending is pronounced three different ways:

  • /d/ after voiced consonants and vowels: played, lived, called, hurried
  • /…™d/ after t or d: wanted, needed, decided, waited

Using Tenses

English has 12 main tenses. Understanding when to use each tense is crucial for clear communication.

Formation:

  • Habits and routines: I drink coffee every morning.
  • Permanent situations: She lives in London.
  • General truths: Water boils at 100¬∞C.

‚ñº

Formation:

  • Positive: I am working; You/We/They are working; He/She/It is working
  • Negative: I'm not working; You/We/They aren't working; He/She/It isn't working
  • Question: Am I working? Are you/we/they working? Is he/she/it working?

Uses:

  • Actions happening now: I'm studying for my exam right now.
  • Temporary situations: She's living with her parents this month.
  • Developing situations: The climate is changing.
  • Future arrangements: We're meeting them tomorrow at 5.
  • Repeated actions (with always, suggesting annoyance): He's always complaining!
Examples:
‚Ä¢ I'm watching TV at the moment.
‚Ä¢ She's not working today.
‚Ä¢ Are you listening to me?
‚Ä¢ They're staying in a hotel this week.

  • Senses: see, hear, smell, taste
  • Emotions: love, hate, like, want, need
  • Mental states: know, understand, believe, remember, forget
  • Possession: have (meaning possess), own, belong
Say: "I know the answer" (NOT "I'm knowing")

Time Expressions:

now, at the moment, currently, right now, today, this week/month/year, Look!, Listen!

‚ñº

  • Negative: I/You/We/They haven't worked; He/She/It hasn't worked

Uses:

  • Life experiences (no specific time): I have visited London. (= at some point in my life)
  • Recent actions with present results: She has lost her keys. (= she doesn't have them now)

  • Past Simple: I went to Paris in 2019. (specific past time)

The Passive

The passive voice is used when we want to focus on the action or the person/thing affected by the action, rather than who performs the action.

Formation and Uses

‚ñº

Basic Formation:

Subject + form of "be" + past participle (+ by + agent)

Active: Shakespeare wrote Hamlet.
Passive: Hamlet was written by Shakespeare.

When to Use the Passive:

  • Unknown agent: My car was stolen. (we don't know who did it)
  • Unimportant agent: The house was built in 1900. (who built it isn't important)
  • Obvious agent: The thief was arrested. (obviously by police)
  • Focus on action/result: The bridge has been repaired.
  • Formal/scientific writing: The experiment was conducted under controlled conditions.

Passive in Different Tenses:

Tense Active Passive
Present Simple They clean the office. The office is cleaned.
Present Continuous They are cleaning the office. The office is being cleaned.
Past Simple They cleaned the office. The office was cleaned.
Past Continuous They were cleaning the office. The office was being cleaned.
Present Perfect They have cleaned the office. The office has been cleaned.
Past Perfect They had cleaned the office. The office had been cleaned.
Future Simple They will clean the office. The office will be cleaned.
Future Perfect They will have cleaned the office. The office will have been cleaned.
Modal Verbs They must clean the office. The office must be cleaned.

Special Cases:

1. Passive with Two Objects:

Active: Someone gave me a book.
Passive 1: I was given a book. (personal passive - more common)
Passive 2: A book was given to me. (impersonal passive)

2. Passive with Reporting Verbs (say, believe, think, know, etc.):

Active: People say that he is very rich.
Passive 1: It is said that he is very rich.
Passive 2: He is said to be very rich.

3. Passive with "get":

In informal English, we can use "get" instead of "be" in the passive:

‚Ä¢ I got fired from my job.

Conditionals

Conditional sentences express hypothetical situations and their consequences. There are four main types of conditionals in English.

Zero Conditional

‚ñº

Structure:

If + present simple, present simple

Use:

General truths, scientific facts, things that are always true

Examples:
‚Ä¢ If you heat water to 100¬∞C, it boils.
‚Ä¢ If I don't sleep enough, I feel tired.
‚Ä¢ Plants die if they don't get water.
‚Ä¢ If you press this button, the machine starts.
Note: You can use "when" instead of "if" in zero conditionals: When you heat water to 100¬∞C, it boils.

First Conditional

‚ñº

Structure:

If + present simple, will + infinitive

Use:

Real or possible situations in the future

Examples:
‚Ä¢ If it rains tomorrow, we will stay home.
‚Ä¢ If you study hard, you'll pass the exam.
‚Ä¢ She won't come if you don't invite her.
‚Ä¢ If I have time, I'll help you.

Variations:

  • Modal verbs instead of "will": If you need help, you can call me.
  • Imperatives: If you see her, tell her to call me.
  • Unless (= if not): I'll go unless it rains. (= I'll go if it doesn't rain)

Second Conditional

‚ñº

Structure:

If + past simple, would + infinitive

Use:

Hypothetical or unlikely situations in the present or future

Examples:
‚Ä¢ If I won the lottery, I would buy a house.
‚Ä¢ If I were you, I wouldn't do that.
‚Ä¢ She would be happier if she had more friends.
‚Ä¢ What would you do if you saw a ghost?
Important Notes:
  • With "be", formal English uses "were" for all persons: If I were rich... (informal: If I was rich...)
  • You can use "could" or "might" instead of "would": If I had more time, I might learn Spanish.
  • This expresses imagination, not reality: If I were the president... (but I'm not)

Third Conditional

‚ñº

Structure:

If + past perfect, would have + past participle

Use:

Hypothetical situations in the past (things that didn't happen)

Examples:
‚Ä¢ If I had studied harder, I would have passed the exam. (but I didn't study hard, so I failed)
‚Ä¢ If we had left earlier, we wouldn't have missed the train.
‚Ä¢ She would have come if you had invited her.
‚Ä¢ What would you have done if you had seen him?

Uses:

  • Regrets: If I had known, I would have told you.
  • Criticism: If you had been more careful, this wouldn't have happened.
  • Imagining different past: If I had been born in America, I would have spoken English as a native.
Variations: You can use "could have" or "might have": If I had tried harder, I might have succeeded.

Modal Verbs

Modal verbs express possibility, permission, ability, obligation, advice, etc. The main modal verbs are: can, could, may, might, must, shall, should, will, would.

Characteristics of Modal Verbs

‚ñº
  • Always followed by infinitive without "to": You must go (NOT must to go)
  • No -s in third person: He can swim (NOT cans)
  • No auxiliary "do" in questions/negatives: Can you help? (NOT Do you can help?)
  • No infinitive or -ing forms: (can't say "to can" or "canning")

Can / Could

‚ñº

Can - Uses:

  • Ability (present): I can speak three languages.
  • Permission (informal): Can I use your phone?
  • Requests (informal): Can you help me?
  • Possibility: It can be very hot in summer.

Could - Uses:

  • Ability (past): When I was young, I could run fast.
  • Polite requests: Could you pass the salt, please?
  • Polite permission: Could I leave early today?
  • Possibility: It could rain tomorrow. (less certain than "might")
  • Suggestions: We could go to the cinema.
Examples:
‚Ä¢ She can play the piano very well.
‚Ä¢ I couldn't find my keys this morning.
‚Ä¢ Could you speak more slowly, please?
‚Ä¢ You could try calling him again.
Note: For ability in the past when something actually happened, use "was/were able to" or "managed to" instead of "could":
  • ‚úÖ I was able to finish the project on time.
  • ‚ùå I could finish the project on time. (sounds like general ability, not a specific achievement)

May / Might

‚ñº

May - Uses:

  • Permission (formal): May I come in?
  • Possibility (50% certain): It may rain later.
  • Wishes (formal): May you have a long and happy life.

Might - Uses:

  • Possibility (less than 50% certain): I might go to the party, I'm not sure.
  • Past possibility: He might have missed the train.
  • Polite suggestions: You might want to see a doctor.
  • Criticism: You might have told me earlier!
Examples:
‚Ä¢ May I ask you a question?
‚Ä¢ She may be at home now.
‚Ä¢ It might snow tonight.
‚Ä¢ They might have left already.
May vs Might: In modern English, the difference is small. "Might" suggests slightly less certainty than "may", but they're often interchangeable.

Must / Have to

‚ñº

Must - Uses:

  • Strong obligation (speaker's opinion): You must wear a seatbelt.
  • Strong advice: You must see this film - it's amazing!
  • Logical deduction (90% certain): She must be tired - she's been working all day.
  • Prohibition (must not): You must not smoke here.

Have to - Uses:

  • External obligation (rules, laws): I have to wear a uniform at work.
  • All tenses: I had to work yesterday. / I'll have to go soon.
Examples:
‚Ä¢ You must try this cake!
‚Ä¢ Students must hand in their assignments by Friday.
‚Ä¢ I have to get up at 6 AM every day.
‚Ä¢ She had to take a taxi because she missed the bus.
Must vs Have to:
  • Must: Speaker's opinion/advice ‚Üí "You must read this book!" (I think it's important)
  • Have to: External rule/obligation ‚Üí "I have to read this book for school." (it's required)
Negatives are different:
  • Must not: Prohibition ‚Üí "You mustn't tell anyone." (it's forbidden)
  • Don't have to: No obligation ‚Üí "You don't have to come." (it's optional)

Should / Ought to

‚ñº

Should - Uses:

  • Advice: You should see a doctor.
  • Expectations: She should be here soon.
  • Criticism (shouldn't have): You shouldn't have said that.
  • Regret (should have): I should have studied harder.

Ought to - Uses:

  • Same as should but more formal: You ought to apologize.
  • Moral obligation: We ought to help those in need.
Examples:
‚Ä¢ You should eat more vegetables.
‚Ä¢ The letter should arrive tomorrow.
‚Ä¢ I should have called you earlier. (regret)
‚Ä¢ You shouldn't have spent so much money. (criticism)

Will / Would

‚ñº

Will - Uses:

  • Future predictions: It will rain tomorrow.
  • Spontaneous decisions: I'll help you with that.
  • Promises: I'll call you tonight.
  • Offers: I'll carry your bag.
  • Requests: Will you close the door?
  • Refusal (won't): The car won't start.

Would - Uses:

  • Polite requests: Would you help me, please?
  • Conditional: If I won, I would buy a car.
  • Past habits: When I was young, I would play in the park every day.
  • Preferences: I would rather stay home.
  • Imaginary situations: It would be nice to live by the sea.
Examples:
‚Ä¢ I'll see you tomorrow.
‚Ä¢ Will you marry me?
‚Ä¢ Would you like some coffee?
‚Ä¢ I would go if I had time.

(Which woman? The one who lives next door - essential information)

‚Ä¢ I lost the book that you gave me.

  • Cannot omit the pronoun when it's the subject

Non-Defining Relative Clauses

‚ñº

What are Non-Defining Relative Clauses?

They provide extra, non-essential information. The sentence makes sense without this information. These clauses are separated by commas.

‚Ä¢ My brother, who lives in London, is a teacher.
(I have one brother - "who lives in London" is extra information)

‚Ä¢ The Taj Mahal, which was built in the 17th century, is in India.
(Everyone knows the Taj Mahal - the clause gives additional information)

Key Differences from Defining Clauses:

  • USE COMMAS before and after the clause
  • Cannot use "that" - must use who/which
  • Cannot omit the relative pronoun
  • Often used with proper nouns or unique things
Examples:
‚Ä¢ Shakespeare, who wrote Hamlet, was born in 1564.
‚Ä¢ My car, which I bought last year, has broken down.
‚Ä¢ Paris, where I was born, is a beautiful city.
‚Ä¢ Monday, when we have our meeting, is a busy day.
Defining vs Non-Defining:

Defining (no commas):
My brother who lives in London is a teacher.
(I have more than one brother - this identifies which one)

Non-Defining (with commas):
My brother, who lives in London, is a teacher.
(I have only one brother - extra information)

Prepositions in Relative Clauses

‚ñº

Two Positions for Prepositions:

1. At the end (informal, more common):

‚Ä¢ The person (who/that) I spoke to was very helpful.
‚Ä¢ That's the house (which/that) I grew up in.
‚Ä¢ The project (which/that) we're working on is important.

2. Before the relative pronoun (formal):

‚Ä¢ The person to whom I spoke was very helpful.
‚Ä¢ That's the house in which I grew up.
‚Ä¢ The project on which we're working is important.
Important: When the preposition comes before the relative pronoun:
  • Use whom (not who) for people
  • Use which (not that) for things
  • Cannot omit the relative pronoun

Punctuation

Correct punctuation is essential for clear communication in written English.

Period / Full Stop (.)

‚ñº

Uses:

  • End of sentences: I went to the shop.
  • Abbreviations: Dr., Mr., Mrs., etc.
  • Decimals: 3.5, 10.99 (but 3,5 in German)

Comma (,)

‚ñº

Uses:

  • Lists: I bought apples, oranges, bananas, and grapes.
  • Before coordinating conjunctions: I went to the shop, but it was closed.
  • After introductory elements: After dinner, we watched TV.
  • Non-defining relative clauses: My brother, who lives in London, is a teacher.
  • Direct speech: She said, "I'm tired."
  • Large numbers: 1,000; 10,000; 1,000,000
Oxford Comma: The comma before "and" in lists is called the Oxford comma. It's optional but can prevent confusion:
  • Without: I love my parents, Lady Gaga and Humpty Dumpty. (suggests parents are Lady Gaga and Humpty Dumpty)
  • With: I love my parents, Lady Gaga, and Humpty Dumpty. (three separate things)

Question Mark (?) and Exclamation Mark (!)

‚ñº

Question Mark (?):

  • Direct questions: Where are you going?
  • NOT with indirect questions: He asked where I was going. (no ?)

Exclamation Mark (!):

  • Strong emotion: What a beautiful day!
  • Commands: Stop!
  • Warnings: Watch out!
Note: Use exclamation marks sparingly in formal writing. Too many can seem unprofessional.

Apostrophe (')

‚ñº

Uses:

  • Possession: John's book, the dog's tail, the children's toys
  • Contractions: don't, isn't, I'm, you're, it's (= it is)
Common Mistakes:
  • ‚ùå Its' ‚Üí ‚úÖ Its (possessive, no apostrophe)
  • ‚ùå It's tail ‚Üí ‚úÖ Its tail (possessive)
  • ‚úÖ It's hot (= It is hot)
  • ‚ùå apple's for sale ‚Üí ‚úÖ apples for sale (plural, no apostrophe)
  • ‚ùå the 1990's ‚Üí ‚úÖ the 1990s (decades, no apostrophe)

Quotation Marks (" ")

‚ñº

Uses:

  • Direct speech: She said, "I'm going home."
  • Quotations: Shakespeare wrote, "To be or not to be."
  • Titles of articles, poems, short stories: I read "The Tell-Tale Heart."
  • Words used in unusual ways: He's very "helpful" (ironic)

  • American: Double quotes first: "Hello," she said.
